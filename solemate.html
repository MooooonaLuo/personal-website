SoleMate

UI/UX · 2018-2019

After a severe lower-extremity injury, how to recover faster and avoid reinjury during the recovery? SoleMate is here to solve this problem. It is a product composed of a smart shoe insole and a mobile app. The smart insole monitors weight applied to the injured foot and provides real time feedback via the mobile app.

SoleMate is developed by SoleMate Solutions, a startup located in San Diego. I was first recruited by them in November 2018 as a graphic designer. I visualized ideas in Arizona State University pitch competition and helped them enter the top 5. By June 2019, my fellow designer and I finished the UI/UX design of the app, and now the team is working on programming and hardwares.

Concept & Background

The Problem

Emily is a 15-year-old girl in the school basketball team. She broke her ankle during the training and was sent to the hospital. Except regular examinations in clinic, she spent most time recovering without monitoring by her physical therapist. She had an urgent need to recover and return to training, but unfortunately, she got reinjured during exercise.


In fact, this kind of case is not occasional. Every year, 5.5 million patients return to hospitals for the exact same problem, and the major cause is poor rehabilitation. Usually, patients with lower-extremity injuries are required by their doctors to apply a certain amount of weight to the injured side, but it is challenging for them to follow the instruction and they almost receive no feedback after clinic. When they walk out of the hospital door, care stops there.



Our Solution

We designed a product composed of two parts, a mobile app and a pair of smart shoe insoles. The mobile app provides patients with real-time feedback of how much weight they are putting on their injured foot, based on data transmitted from pressure sensors on the smart insoles. Thereby, patients are able to adjust the way they walk. The app will also summarize patients' activity data, so that physical therapist can give them useful advice based on the summary.

The App Design Process

We has partnership with Rady Children's Hospital and receives professional advice from the doctors. In this way, we are able to comprehensively understand the process of treatment and to build an app that satisfy users’ need in the real world.


Storyboard



Information Architecture

The trickiest thing about this step is to decide what to include in Onboarding. When people use this app for the first time, they need to understand the use of this app and enter their personal information as well. However, this is just too much for Onboarding and we don’t want to burden our users with so many tasks at once. Then we decided not to include explanation of this app in Onboarding and to provide clear instructions when users actually start to use the app. Another reason we made this decision is that in most cases, the product would be introduced by doctors to patients, and there is no need for the app to introduce itself again.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

Usability Testing

So far, we have done two usability tests, one with low-fidelity prototype and one with high-fidelity prototype. For each time, we selected 6-8 participants. Half of them have experienced lower-extremity injuries before, and the other half have not. In this way, we got insights from participants who are familiar with the rehab process and those who are not as well.

We found several problems after the first round. The first problem is that the information on Activity page is a little overwhelming. So we split the information on that page and put them in two "cards," the first one showing statistics and the second one showing the current rehabilitation plan. We put "percentage within target range" to the second page of the first card, since this information has lower priority.

Inspired by the participants, we added on Tips to the Result page. Although numbers are important in terms of statistics, users have no idea how to interpret the numbers. In contrary, textual tips about how to improve users' rehabilitation activities are and more straighforward and helpful for users.

Visual Design

When making decision on visual design, we thought of SoleMate as a person, and each of us chose a few words to describe the characteristics of it. In general, we considered it to be tranquil, professional, and friendly. Then we followed these characteristics to make decision on the colors, shapes, fonts, and illustration styles.


Feature Highlights

Onboarding

Users create new accounts, put in their personal information, and add rehabilitation plan. The rehabilitation plan can have multiple sessions. Users will be asked to put in their goal body weight (the percentage of body weight that users should apply to their injured lower extremities per doctors' instructions) when adding each session.



Statistics & Device Status

The Home page has two parts, users' activity statistics and the status of smart insoles. On the Activity page, the average percentages of body weight applied by users are displayed in linear charts. Users can also find information about their current rehabilitation session. On the Insole page, the battery condition and Bluetooth connection status are provided.



Real-Time Feedback Testing

This function is aimed to help users to learn the appropriate way of walking. If users apply weight to their injured lower extremities that exceeds the target range, they will receive sonic feedbacks immediately. The summary and tips will be provided after testings.

Takeaways

Designing and developing SoleMate in a team is very inspiring and fruitful for me. I realized that the most significant qualities for a designer is the ability to always clearly see the ultimate design goal and the willingness to actively communicate with the rest of the team. The two founders of SoleMate Solutions are very confident about what they want for every step of the project. At first, I just did whatever they wanted me to do. But later I realized that they were not always right and that my insights as a designer are valuable to achieve the goal. I was not helping them to finish a bunch of trivial tasks but to reach their ultimate goal.
